Nexus is a contextual multi-armed bandit. Its objective is to maximize a scalar reward signal derived from user engagement — opens, conversions, donations, and reading-plan completions — across push, email, and in-app channels. Over the next ten minutes we'll walk the full pipeline: how a user becomes a vector, how vectors become personas, how three different bandit algorithms choose a message, how engagement becomes reward, and how we prove the whole thing actually works.

Start with representation. Every user in Nexus is a point in a ten-dimensional feature space. The dimensions fall into two bands. Dimensions zero through five are behavioral, derived from Nexus's own decision and conversion logs. Dimensions six through nine are semantic, synced from YouVersion through Hightouch reverse ETL.

The behavioral band: dimension zero is push conversion rate — push conversions over push sent. Dimension one is the same for email. These are deliberately not normalized against each other, because absolute channel preference matters: forty percent push and two percent email is a very different user than fifteen and fourteen. Dimensions two, three, and four are temporal ratios — the share of engagement that falls between five and eleven AM, between five and ten PM, and on weekends. They are independent windows, so they don't sum to one; a night owl sits near zero on all three. Dimension five is the overall conversion rate, a global responsiveness signal.

The semantic band: dimension six is a recency score, a linear decay from one to zero over ninety days since last open. Dimension seven is the giving tier — zero for none, one-half for a giver, one for a recurring sower. Dimension eight is spiritual depth, a composite that averages five normalized signals: reading streak, plans finished, prayer sessions, scripture sessions, and badges, with log-scaling on the heavy-tailed ones. Dimension nine is engagement frequency, log-scaled sends per week. Every dimension is normalized to roughly zero to one.

This replaced an older forty-four dimension layout, where a twenty-four-bin hourly histogram and a seven-bin day-of-week histogram were heavily correlated and dominated the distance metric. Collapsing them into a handful of interpretable ratios gives a denser vector, better convergence, and cleaner arm separation.

And distance matters because Nexus compares users by cosine similarity, not Euclidean distance. Cosine measures the angle between two vectors — it's invariant to magnitude. Because every dimension is non-negative, cosine similarity lands in zero to one, and we define cosine distance as one minus that. A user with a thousand sends at thirty percent points in the same direction as one with ten sends at thirty percent. New users have an all-zero vector — undefined under cosine — so they're skipped for persona assignment and fall back to a global prior until their first conversion lands.

Now clustering. Nexus discovers personas with k-means plus plus over cosine distance. Standard k-means seeds centroids at random and often falls into poor local minima. K-means plus plus seeds them far apart: pick the first centroid at random, then sample each next one with probability proportional to its squared distance from the nearest chosen centroid. That spreads the seeds across the space and converges faster.

Then it iterates. The assignment step puts each user in the cluster whose centroid maximizes cosine similarity. The update step recomputes each centroid as the mean of its members. It repeats until no assignment changes, or up to a hundred iterations — usually fifteen to thirty in practice.

But how many personas? Nexus doesn't fix k. It runs k-means for every k in a range, typically three to fifteen, and scores each with the silhouette coefficient. For a user, a is the mean distance to its own cluster and b the mean distance to the nearest other cluster; the silhouette is b minus a over the max of the two. Average it over all users and you get a number between minus one and one. Nexus keeps the k with the highest mean silhouette, provided it clears a floor of zero point two five — below that, the data doesn't cluster reliably and the run is discarded, leaving existing personas intact. And because initialization is stochastic, it does five stability runs per k and keeps the best. A rule-based classifier can then override the cluster assignment for high-signal archetypes — like tagging a lapsed user with a prior plan completion as a Re-engager.

The payoff of clustering: each persona gets its own independent set of arm statistics. Variant A can beat Variant B for one segment while the reverse holds for another.

Now the decision layer — three algorithms. The default is Thompson Sampling. Each arm carries a Beta distribution with two parameters: alpha, accumulated weighted success, and beta, accumulated failure. The expected value is alpha over alpha plus beta, and the variance shrinks as the counts grow. At decision time, draw one sample from every arm's Beta and send the highest draw. That single rule is both exploration and exploitation: high-value arms draw high most of the time, but a wide, uncertain arm occasionally draws higher and earns a send.

New arms are initialized pessimistically, at Beta one, thirty — an expected value near three percent, matching real push click-through. A uniform Beta one, one prior would imply fifty percent and flood unproven variants with traffic; the pessimistic prior makes a new arm earn its place over about thirty sends. Under the hood, Beta samples are generated from two Gamma samples using the Marsaglia-Tsang method with a Box-Muller normal — order-one amortized, no external math library. Selection scores can also be deflated by a recency penalty so the same variant doesn't dominate one user's history.

The second algorithm is LinUCB — a contextual bandit. Thompson Sampling ignores the feature vector; LinUCB conditions on it. Each arm keeps an inverse design matrix A-inverse, ten by ten, starting at the identity, and a reward vector b. The estimated weights are theta equals A-inverse times b, and the score for a user with feature vector x is theta-transpose x — the exploit term — plus alpha times the square root of x-transpose A-inverse x — the explore term. That second term is large when x points in a direction the arm hasn't seen, so unfamiliar contexts get an uncertainty bonus. Updating a full matrix inverse would be order d-cubed every step; instead the Sherman-Morrison formula does a rank-one update in order d-squared, keeping each update within microseconds even across thousands of users per cron batch. Use Thompson Sampling when the bandit is already persona-segmented; reach for LinUCB when fine-grained, within-segment personalization matters.

The third is Epsilon-Greedy, the legacy fallback. With probability epsilon — default ten percent — pick a random arm; otherwise pick the highest empirical win rate. Simple and auditable, but its exploration is blind: even a near-certain winner keeps donating epsilon of its traffic to random arms. Epsilon decays by a factor each run toward a floor of one percent.

Next: how engagement becomes reward. Goals are tiered. A plan completion sits in the best tier at plus ten; a plan start at plus seven; a Bible open at plus five; no conversion is neutral; disabling push is the worst, at minus ten. The raw reward is the tier base times a value weight, then divided by a hundred and clamped to minus one through one. So a best-tier conversion at weight one becomes zero point one — a modest nudge that keeps any single event from dominating, and keeps the parameters in scale with the decay rate. The update is asymmetric: a positive reward adds its fractional value to alpha, while any failure adds exactly one to beta. Nexus learns how good its successes are, but only how often it fails. Statistics live at two levels — a shared persona prior and a per-user posterior — blended by observation count, so cold-start users lean on the persona while heavy users lean on their own history.

Attribution connects send to outcome. Standard goals use forty-eight hours; long-horizon goals like plan completion use thirty days; disabling push applies a ninety-day retroactive penalty to every recent send. And after each update, alpha and beta are both decayed by zero point nine nine — a half-life around sixty-nine updates, so the model stays present-weighted without ever fully forgetting.

Timing matters too. From each user's last-seen timestamp, Nexus derives a preferred send hour, then schedules ten minutes earlier — just before the next likely session, since last-seen marks session end, not start. If there's no signal or the time has passed, it falls back to a default hour and advances to tomorrow, because Braze rejects past timestamps. A timing lottery then admits only users whose preferred time is near the next batch, spreading sends across the day instead of one burst.

Finally — does it work? Lift is the treatment conversion rate minus a uniform-random baseline, over that baseline. To test it, Nexus runs a two-proportion z-test: pool the proportions, compute the standard error, and reject the null when the absolute z exceeds one point nine six. It reports a ninety-five percent confidence interval from the unpooled error; if that interval excludes zero, the lift is significant. But Thompson Sampling concentrates traffic on winners, which breaks the equal-split assumption behind the classic test — so wide intervals at low sample sizes are shown honestly, and the right move is to let the bandit run until they narrow, not to poll for early significance.

That's the full pipeline. Represent, cluster, choose, reward, decay, schedule, and measure — every hour, across every channel. In production, a thirty-four percent lift over random. That's Nexus.
