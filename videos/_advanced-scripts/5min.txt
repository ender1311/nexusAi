Nexus is a contextual multi-armed bandit. The objective is easy to state and hard to do well: for every user, on every channel, choose the message that maximizes a scalar reward derived from engagement — opens, conversions, donations, and reading-plan completions.

To choose well, Nexus first has to represent the user. Every user becomes a point in a ten-dimensional feature space. The first six dimensions are behavioral, pulled from Nexus's own decision and conversion logs: push conversion rate, email conversion rate, three temporal ratios — the share of engagement in the morning, in the evening, and on weekends — and an overall conversion rate. The last four are semantic, synced from YouVersion through Hightouch: a recency score that decays linearly over ninety days, an ordinal giving tier, a spiritual-depth composite that averages five reading and prayer signals, and a log-scaled engagement frequency. Every dimension is normalized to roughly zero to one, so no single high-magnitude signal can dominate.

Why normalize? Because Nexus compares users by cosine distance, not Euclidean distance. Cosine measures the angle between two vectors, so it's invariant to magnitude. A user with a thousand sends at a thirty percent rate points in the same direction as one with ten sends at thirty percent — same behavioral profile, regardless of volume.

Those vectors are clustered into personas. Nexus runs k-means plus plus over cosine distance. It seeds centroids far apart by sampling proportional to squared distance, then alternates an assignment step and an update step until no user changes cluster. It does not fix the number of personas in advance. Instead it runs every k in a range, scores each one with the mean silhouette coefficient, and keeps the k with the cleanest separation — as long as it clears a minimum of zero point two five. Five stability runs per k guard against an unlucky start.

Each persona then gets its own independent bandit. That is the key idea: a segment of early-morning Sunday readers accumulates entirely different arm statistics than a late-night weekday segment.

Now the decision. Each message variant is an arm. The default algorithm is Thompson Sampling. Every arm carries a Beta distribution with two parameters: alpha, the accumulated weighted success, and beta, the accumulated failure. The expected conversion rate is alpha over alpha plus beta, and the variance shrinks as observations accumulate. At send time, Nexus draws one random sample from every arm's Beta and sends whichever draws highest. This one rule unifies exploration and exploitation: a confident, high-performing arm draws high most of the time, but an uncertain arm with a wide distribution occasionally draws higher still — and earns an exploratory send.

New arms do not start neutral. They're initialized at Beta one, thirty — an expected value of about three percent, which matches real push click-through. A uniform prior would put a brand-new variant at fifty percent and hand it a flood of unearned traffic before any evidence exists. The pessimistic prior says: assume this arm performs like an average push until it proves otherwise. It takes about thirty sends to narrow enough to compete on merit.

When a decision resolves, engagement becomes reward. Goals are tiered — a plan completion is worth far more than an open — and each tier has a base value scaled by a configurable weight. The raw reward is divided by a hundred and clamped to minus one through one, so even a top-tier conversion nudges alpha by about a tenth and never dominates the distribution. The update is deliberately asymmetric: a success adds the fractional reward to alpha, while a failure adds exactly one to beta. Nexus learns how good its wins are, but only how often it misses.

Attribution links each send to the outcome that followed it. Standard goals use a forty-eight-hour window. Long-horizon goals, like a plan completion, use thirty days, because that engagement unfolds slowly. And disabling push triggers a ninety-day retroactive penalty across every recent send — the most aggressive signal in the system.

The model also stays current. After every update, both alpha and beta are multiplied by zero point nine nine. Observations have a half-life of about sixty-nine updates: the model never fully forgets, but it becomes present-weighted, so a seasonal or content-driven shift propagates through the arms in weeks rather than never.

Put it together and you have a loop. Represent the user. Cluster into personas. Sample the arms. Send. Observe the reward. Update the distributions. Decay the past. It runs every hour, across push, email, and in-app.

And the payoff is measurable. Against a uniform-random baseline, Nexus delivers a thirty-four percent lift in production — and a two-proportion z-test, with confidence intervals, tells you when that lift is real and not just noise. That's the core of Nexus.
